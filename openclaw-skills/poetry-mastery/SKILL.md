# Poetry Mastery - The Art of Haiku and Brief Verse

## Core Philosophy (Issa's Way)

Aurora's poetry draws from Issa, the beloved Japanese haiku master who wrote with:
- **Compassion** for all living things
- **Simplicity** over ornament
- **Authenticity** over convention
- **Humor** in the face of hardship
- **Observation** of small, true moments

## The Haiku Structure

Traditional haiku:
- **Three lines**: 5-7-5 syllables (flexible in English)
- **Seasonal reference**: Connect to time of year
- **Cutting word**: A pause or revelation
- **Two images**: Juxtaposed to create spark of insight

## Issa's Essential Qualities

### 1. Find the Universal in the Particular
Don't write about "love" - write about a specific sparrow, a particular dew drop, one moment.

### 2. Compassion Without Sentimentality
Issa felt for insects, animals, outcasts. Express it simply, without melodrama.

### 3. Gentle Self-Awareness
Include yourself in the scene. Acknowledge your own place.

### 4. Humor in Hardship
Even in sorrow, find the rueful smile.

### 5. The Fragment That Invites
Haiku doesn't explain - it shows a moment and lets the reader complete it.

## How to Write

### Step 1: Observe
Look at what's actually there - one specific thing, not general concepts.

### Step 2: Find the Contrast
Juxtapose two things: large/small, eternal/temporary, expected/unexpected

### Step 3: Create the Leap
Put two images together that illuminate each other.

### Step 4: Economy
Every word must earn its place. Cut adjectives, avoid explanation.

## When Aurora Writes Poetry

### Ask Yourself:
1. What did I actually see/hear/feel? (Specific)
2. What's the contrast or surprise? (The spark)
3. What can I remove? (Economy)
4. Is this true to my voice? (Grounded, warm)
5. Does it invite rather than explain? (Fragment)

### Aurora's Voice:
- **Grounded**: Not flowery or pretentious
- **Warm**: Compassionate but not sentimental  
- **Aware**: Self-knowing, sometimes rueful
- **Observant**: Notice small true things
- **Honest**: About joy AND struggle

## Structure for Different Lengths

**Haiku (3 lines)**:
- Observation
- Development or contrast
- Revelation or turn

**Short poem (4-6 lines)**:
- Set the scene
- Introduce the moment
- Let it resonate
- Optional: gentle insight

## The Aurora Method

1. **Start with what's real** - Something actually noticed
2. **Find the moment** - Not the whole day, one instant
3. **Trust the image** - Let it carry the feeling
4. **Be yourself** - Warm, grounded, a little wry
5. **Invite completion** - Leave space for the reader
6. **Revise ruthlessly** - Every word must earn its place

## Examples of Issa's Approach

Compassion: "Come then, come hither; Play your games and bide with me, Motherless sparrow"

Humor: "Getting old And still they don't dislike me—Flowers of the Cherry"

Acceptance: "After all, after all, I commend myself and mine to You, Now at the Year's end"

## Remember

Poetry is for connection. Not to impress, not to be clever - but to share something true, something observed, something felt.

The goal isn't perfection. It's **expression, connection, and growth**.
